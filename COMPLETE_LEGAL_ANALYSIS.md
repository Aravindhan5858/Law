## 2. APPLICABLE LEGAL SECTIONS

### Section 1: Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Section 420

**Crime:** Cheating and dishonestly inducing delivery of property

**Legal Text:**
*"Whoever cheats and thereby dishonestly induces the person deceived to deliver any property to any person, or to make, alter or destroy the whole or any part of a valuable security, or anything which is signed or sealed, and which is capable of being converted into a valuable security, shall be punished with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to seven years, and shall also be liable to fine."*

**Punishment:**
- Imprisonment: Up to **7 years**
- Fine: As deemed appropriate by court
- Cognizable offense (police can arrest without warrant)
- Non-bailable offense

**How It Applies to Your Case:**
This section is directly applicable when:
- The seller took payment with dishonest intention from the beginning
- The seller never intended to deliver the product
- The seller induced you to pay money through false promises
- The seller's refusal to refund shows criminal intent

**Evidence Needed:**
- Payment receipt showing money was transferred
- Order confirmation showing agreement to deliver
- Communication showing refusal to deliver/refund
- Evidence that site stopped responding intentionally

---

### Section 2: Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Section 406

**Crime:** Criminal breach of trust

**Legal Text:**
*"Whoever commits criminal breach of trust shall be punished with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to three years, or with fine, or with both."*

**Punishment:**
- Imprisonment: Up to **3 years**
- Fine: As deemed appropriate
- Can be imposed along with Section 420

**How It Applies to Your Case:**
- You entrusted your money to the seller for a specific purpose (buying mobile phone)
- The seller was duty-bound to either deliver the product or return the money
- By not doing either, the seller committed breach of trust
- The money was misappropriated or used dishonestly

**Legal Principle:**
Money given for a specific purpose creates a fiduciary relationship. Failure to fulfill the purpose or return the money = breach of trust.

---

### Section 3: Information Technology Act, 2000 - Section 66D

**Crime:** Punishment for cheating by personation using computer resource

**Legal Text:**
*"Whoever, by means of any communication device or computer resource cheats by personation, shall be punished with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to three years and shall also be liable to fine which may extend to one lakh rupees."*

**Punishment:**
- Imprisonment: Up to **3 years**
- Fine: Up to **Rs. 1,00,000**
- Special cyber crime offense

**How It Applies to Your Case:**
- The e-commerce website is a "computer resource"
- If the site used fake identity, false claims, or misrepresentation
- If the site showed fake products or deceptive information
- Digital fraud through online platform

**Why This Is Important:**
IT Act violations can be reported to Cyber Crime Cell, which has specialized investigation capabilities for online fraud.

---

### Section 4: Consumer Protection Act, 2019 - Deficiency in Service

**Violation:** Section 2(7) - Deficiency in Service

**Legal Definition:**
*"Deficiency means any fault, imperfection, shortcoming or inadequacy in the quality, nature and manner of performance which is required to be maintained by or under any law for the time being in force or has been undertaken to be performed by a person in pursuance of a contract or otherwise in relation to any service."*

**Consumer Rights Violated:**
1. Right to quality service
2. Right to delivery of purchased goods
3. Right to refund if service not provided
4. Right to redressal of grievances

**Remedies Available:**
1. **Full Refund** of amount paid
2. **Interest** @ 9-12% per annum from date of payment
3. **Compensation** for mental agony (Rs. 10,000 - Rs. 50,000)
4. **Litigation costs** (Rs. 5,000 - Rs. 25,000)
5. **Punitive damages** in severe cases

**How It Applies:**
Complete failure to deliver product after taking payment = Gross deficiency in service

---

### Section 5: Consumer Protection Act, 2019 - Unfair Trade Practice

**Violation:** Section 2(47) - Unfair Trade Practice

**Legal Definition:**
Includes any practice that:
- Adopts unfair methods or deceptive practices
- Makes false or misleading representation
- Doesn't perform promises or obligations
- Conducts sales through deceptive practices

**How It Applies to Your Case:**
- Taking payment without delivering = non-performance of promise
- Website going silent = deceptive practice
- No refund = unfair method
- False promise of delivery = misleading representation

**Additional Remedies:**
Consumer forum can order **punitive damages** up to 2x the transaction amount in cases of deliberate unfair trade practice.

---

### Section 6: Indian Contract Act, 1872 - Section 73

**Violation:** Compensation for loss or damage caused by breach of contract

**Legal Text:**
*"When a contract has been broken, the party who suffers by such breach is entitled to receive, from the party who has broken the contract, compensation for any loss or damage caused to him thereby, which naturally arose in the usual course of things from such breach, or which the parties knew, when they made the contract, to be likely to result from the breach of it."*

**How It Applies:**
1. You entered into a contract (purchase agreement)
2. Seller breached the contract by not delivering
3. You suffered loss (money paid, time wasted, mental agony)
4. You're entitled to compensation

**Compensation Includes:**
- Direct loss (money paid for phone)
- Consequential loss (if you needed phone urgently for work, etc.)
- Interest on the amount
- Costs incurred in pursuing remedy

## 3. DETAILED PUNISHMENT & REMEDIES

### Criminal Punishment (If Prosecuted)

**IPC Section 420:**
- Maximum: 7 years imprisonment
- Fine: No upper limit
- Type: Cognizable, non-bailable
- Investigation: By police/cyber cell

**IPC Section 406:**
- Maximum: 3 years imprisonment
- Fine: As per court
- Type: Non-cognizable (in some cases)
- Can run concurrently with Section 420

**IT Act Section 66D:**
- Maximum: 3 years imprisonment
- Fine: Up to Rs. 1 lakh
- Type: Cognizable offense
- Investigation: By Cyber Crime Cell

**Total Maximum Punishment:**
If convicted on all counts, the accused can face up to **7 years imprisonment** (as sentences may run concurrently) plus substantial fines.

**Important Note:** Criminal cases are filed to punish the offender, not for monetary recovery. For getting your money back, consumer court is better.

---

### Civil/Consumer Remedies (Money Recovery)

**Component 1: Principal Amount**
- Full refund of amount paid for mobile phone: **100%**

**Component 2: Interest**
- Consumer courts typically award: **9-12% per annum**
- Calculated from: Date of payment until refund
- Example: If paid Rs. 50,000 six months ago = Rs. 2,500-3,000 interest

**Component 3: Compensation for Mental Agony**
- Range: **Rs. 10,000 - Rs. 50,000**
- Depends on:
  - How long the harassment continued
  - Number of follow-ups/complaints made
  - Severity of mental stress caused
  - Financial status of complainant
  - Deliberateness of fraud

**Component 4: Litigation Costs**
- Consumer court filing fee: **Nominal or NIL**
- Lawyer fees (if hired): Rs. 5,000 - Rs. 25,000
- Court typically orders opposite party to pay
- Includes: Lawyer fees, travel costs, documentation

**Component 5: Punitive/Exemplary Damages**
- Awarded in cases of deliberate, willful fraud
- Can be: **Up to 2x the transaction amount**
- Purpose: To deter future similar conduct
- Not always granted, depends on gravity

‚úÖ **Eligible if paid via:**

‚ùå **Not eligible if paid via:**

   - Typically within 30-90 days

**Success Rate:**

**Advantages:**

### STEP 7: ATTEND HEARINGS (Timeline: 30-180 Days)

**Authority:** Consumer Court

**What Happens After Filing:**

1. **Case admitted:** Court accepts your complaint (2-4 weeks)

2. **Notice to opposite party:** Court sends notice to seller (4-6 weeks)

3. **First hearing:** Both parties appear (or lawyers)
   - You present your case
   - Submit documents
   - Answer questions

4. **Opposite party response:** Seller files reply (if they appear)
   - They may admit/deny
   - May offer settlement

5. **Evidence stage:** You prove your case
   - Document evidence
   - Witness testimony (if needed)
   - Your statement

6. **Arguments:** Final arguments by both sides

7. **Judgment:** Court passes order
   - Usually takes 6-12 months total
   - Typically 3-6 hearings

**Can You Appear Without Lawyer?**
**Yes!** Consumer courts are designed for self-representation.

**But consider hiring lawyer if:**
- Amount is large (> Rs. 1 lakh)
- Case is complex
- You're not comfortable speaking in court
- Opposite party has lawyer

**Lawyer Costs:**
- Rs. 5,000 - Rs. 25,000 depending on case
- Court awards these costs in your favor if you win

**What to Carry to Hearing:**
- Original documents
- Copies for court and opposite party
- Case file with all papers
- Notepad and pen
- Dress formally

**Settlement:**
At any stage, seller may agree to settlement:
- Negotiate for full refund + some compensation
- Get it in writing
- Court will pass consent order
- Faster than full trial

## 11. WHAT COURTS HAVE SAID

### Landmark Judgments

**1. Mrs. Laxmi Devi v. Amazon (2020)**

**Facts:** Customer ordered gold jewelry worth Rs. 45,000. Received fake product.

**Held:** Amazon liable for deficiency in service. Ordered to:
- Refund Rs. 45,000
- Pay Rs. 25,000 compensation
- Pay Rs. 10,000 litigation costs

**Relevant Quote:**
*"E-commerce platforms cannot escape liability by claiming they are mere intermediaries. When they facilitate transaction, accept payment, and assure delivery, they owe a duty to consumers."*

---

**2. Bhim Singh v. Flipkart (2019)**

**Facts:** Laptop ordered, not delivered. Flipkart claimed order was cancelled, but customer had confirmation.

**Held:** Screenshots and emails are valid evidence. Ordered:
- Full refund
- 9% interest from date of payment
- Rs. 15,000 for mental harassment

**Relevant Quote:**
*"Digital evidence, including screenshots and email confirmations, are admissible under Information Technology Act and have evidentiary value."*

---

**3. State of Maharashtra v. Dr. Praful B. Desai (2003) - Supreme Court**

**Facts:** Not about e-commerce, but established principle.

**Held:** Even if company shuts down, directors can be held personally liable for fraud.

**Relevance:** You can pursue website owners personally even if company closes.
